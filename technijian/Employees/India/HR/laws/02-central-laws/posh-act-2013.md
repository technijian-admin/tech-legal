# Sexual Harassment of Women at Workplace (Prevention, Prohibition and Redressal) Act, 2013 — "POSH Act"

**Status:** In force; **not** subsumed under any of the four labour codes. Applies independently.
**Last verified:** 2026-05-06.

---

## Why it survives the codification

The four labour codes (2019/2020) consolidate **wage, social-security, industrial-relations, and OSH** legislation. The POSH Act addresses a different subject (gender-based workplace harassment) and remains in force unchanged.

POSH compliance is **mandatory** for any employer with **≥10 employees** in India, regardless of the four-codes status.

---

## Scope and definitions

### Workplace (§2(o))

Very broad. Includes:
- Any establishment, enterprise, institution, office, branch.
- Government, private, public-sector undertakings.
- Hospitals, sports institutes, stadia.
- Dwelling place / house (if domestic worker).
- **Any place visited by the employee arising out of or during the course of employment**, including transportation provided by employer for travel.

For Technijian: covers offices, client sites, off-site events, work travel, work-from-home (extended interpretation), and Technijian-arranged transport.

### Aggrieved woman (§2(a))

Includes:
- Women employees of all kinds (regular, contractual, daily-wage, ad-hoc, voluntary, intern).
- Visitors / clients to the workplace.
- Domestic workers in a residence.

Crucially, the act protects women **regardless of whether they are direct employees** of the respondent.

### Sexual harassment (§2(n))

Includes any unwelcome:
- (i) Physical contact and advances;
- (ii) Demand or request for sexual favours;
- (iii) Sexually coloured remarks;
- (iv) Showing pornography;
- (v) Any other unwelcome physical, verbal or non-verbal conduct of sexual nature.

### "Sexual harassment" also expanded by §3(2) to include conduct affecting:
- Implied or explicit promise of preferential treatment.
- Implied or explicit threat of detrimental treatment.
- Implied or explicit threat about employment status.
- Interference with work or creation of intimidating / offensive / hostile work environment.
- Humiliating treatment likely to affect health or safety.

## Mandatory Internal Committee (IC / formerly ICC)

### Constitution (§4)

Every employer of an establishment with **≥10 employees** must, by written order, constitute an Internal Committee (IC) at every office or administrative unit.

### Composition (§4(2))

| Role | Required |
|---|---|
| **Presiding Officer** | A woman employed at a senior level. If no senior woman is available, nominated from another office of the employer. |
| **At least 2 members** | From among employees, committed to the cause of women, or having experience in social work or legal knowledge. |
| **One external member** | From an NGO, association committed to women's cause, or person familiar with sexual harassment issues. |

**At least 50% of total members must be women.**

Term of each member: **3 years** (renewable).

### Practical implication for Technijian

Each Technijian Indian office (Chandigarh, Panchkula) must have its own IC. If Technijian's HQ for India is in one location and a branch in another, there must be an IC at each "administrative unit". A single IC for both does **not** satisfy §4 if they are separate workplaces under different administrative supervision.

External member: usually engaged on a per-meeting / per-case retainer (~₹5,000 – ₹15,000 per session) from a women's-rights NGO or law firm.

## Complaint and Inquiry Process

### Filing complaint (§9)

- In writing, **within 3 months** of the incident (extendable up to another 3 months for sufficient reasons).
- 6 copies + supporting documents + names of witnesses.

### Conciliation (§10) — optional

- Before initiating formal inquiry, IC may attempt **conciliation** at the request of the aggrieved woman.
- No monetary settlement may be on this basis.

### Formal inquiry (§11)

- IC has powers of a civil court (summoning witnesses, requiring production of documents).
- Inquiry must be **completed within 90 days**.
- Both parties heard; principles of natural justice followed.
- Confidentiality preserved.

### Report (§13)

- IC must furnish report to employer within **10 days** of completion of inquiry.
- Findings: complaint proved / not proved.
- Recommendations for action.

### Action by employer (§13(3))

If complaint **proved**, employer must take action within **60 days** of receipt of report:
- Treat the conduct as misconduct under service rules.
- Disciplinary action (warning, censure, withholding promotion, withholding increments, termination, etc.).
- Compensation to aggrieved woman (deductible from respondent's salary or paid by respondent).
- Counselling / community service for respondent.

If complaint **not proved**, no action against respondent. False or malicious complaint → action against the complainant (rare; protective of bona fide complaints).

### Compensation factors (§15)

When determining compensation:
- Mental trauma, pain, suffering.
- Loss of career opportunity.
- Medical expenses.
- Income & financial status of respondent.
- Feasibility of lump-sum or installment.

## Employer Duties (§19) — non-negotiable

Every employer must:

- (a) Provide a **safe working environment**.
- (b) Display at conspicuous place: penal consequences of sexual harassment + IC composition / contact.
- (c) Organise **regular workshops and awareness programmes** to sensitise employees.
- (d) Organise **orientation programmes** for IC members.
- (e) Provide **necessary facilities** to the IC for conducting inquiries.
- (f) Assist in securing attendance of respondent and witnesses.
- (g) **Make available all information** to the IC related to the complaint.
- (h) Treat sexual harassment as misconduct under service rules.
- (i) Monitor timely submission of reports by IC.

---

## Annual Report (§21)

The IC must prepare an **annual report** of:
- Number of complaints received and disposed.
- Pending complaints over 90 days.
- Number of workshops / awareness programmes.
- Action taken by employer.

Submitted to the District Officer (concerned authority of the state government).

The employer must also include details in the **annual report or annual returns** of the company / establishment (mandated by Companies Act 2013 for companies + many state-level S&E Act returns).

---

## Confidentiality (§16)

- Identity and address of aggrieved woman, respondent, witnesses, contents of complaint, inquiry proceedings, recommendations and action — **shall not be published, communicated or made known to public, press or media**.
- Penalty for breach: **₹5,000** + disciplinary action.

---

## Penalties (§§ 26)

| Default | Penalty |
|---|---|
| Failure to constitute IC | Fine **up to ₹50,000** |
| Failure to comply with §19 employer duties | Fine **up to ₹50,000** |
| Repeated offence | **Twice the punishment** + cancellation of license / withdrawal of registration |

For listed companies, repeated POSH non-compliance can also impact corporate governance ratings and ESG scoring.

## Domestic workers / contract / interns

POSH applies even where the woman is:
- A contractor's employee at Technijian's workplace.
- A vendor / consultant / intern.
- A visiting client representative.

The IC of **Technijian** (the establishment where the harassment occurred) handles the complaint, regardless of the woman's formal employer.

---

## Local Committee (§5)

Where the establishment has **<10 employees**, or the complaint is against the employer himself, the aggrieved woman may file with the **Local Committee** constituted by the District Officer.

Less relevant to Technijian (≥10 employees), but worth knowing if a complaint comes in against the founder / managing director — the woman has the option to bypass the IC and approach the Local Committee.

## Recent jurisprudential trends (2024–2026)

- Courts have emphasised **strict adherence to procedural fairness** in IC inquiries — avoid pre-judgement; provide respondent full opportunity.
- Courts have struck down terminations where the IC inquiry was procedurally defective.
- Compensation awards in proven cases have ranged from ₹1 lakh to ₹50 lakh depending on severity, mental trauma, and organisational position.
- Multiple companies have been fined under §26 for **failing to constitute / properly compose** the IC, even when no complaint had been filed.
- The Supreme Court (Aureliano Fernandes v. State of Goa, 2023) directed all State Governments and Union Territories to:
  - Conduct surveys of POSH compliance in all establishments;
  - Maintain a public list of registered IC members;
  - Take action against non-compliant employers.
