**The Duty of Technology Competence**

**ABA Model Rule 1.1 (Comment 8)** and **California Rule of Professional Conduct 1.6(c)** require lawyers to "make reasonable efforts to prevent inadvertent or unauthorized disclosure of, or unauthorized access to, information relating to the representation of a client." **ABA Formal Opinion 477R** (2017) explicitly frames this duty around email security — requiring attorneys to evaluate the nature of client information, attack vectors, and the cost/efficacy of protective measures.

In practical terms, that means:

- Phishing attacks that expose client communications are a professional-responsibility incident, not just an IT problem.
- A business email compromise that results in wire-fraud loss can trigger both a State Bar complaint and a legal-malpractice claim.
- Reasonable efforts today includes AI-driven phishing detection — it is no longer considered a premium or optional control.
