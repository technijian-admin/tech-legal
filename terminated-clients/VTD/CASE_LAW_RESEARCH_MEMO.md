## 1. BREACH OF WRITTEN CONTRACT — ELEMENTS

**Standard elements** (apply to First Cause of Action in Demand):

> "[T]he elements of a cause of action for breach of contract are (1) the existence of the contract, (2) plaintiff's performance or excuse for nonperformance, (3) defendant's breach, and (4) the resulting damages to the plaintiff."
> *Oasis West Realty, LLC v. Goldman* (2011) 51 Cal.4th 811, 821.

See also:
- *Reichert v. General Ins. Co.* (1968) 68 Cal.2d 822, 830 (breach of contract elements).
- *Coles v. Glaser* (2016) 2 Cal.App.5th 384, 391 (party's failure to perform a contractual duty constitutes breach).
- *CDF Firefighters v. Maldonado* (2008) 158 Cal.App.4th 1226, 1239 (where the contract is in writing, only the contract's express terms control absent ambiguity).

**Application:** Technijian executed the Agreement, performed (continuous IT support documented through ticketing, monthly invoicing accepted and paid for ~27 months), and was damaged when Vintage Design terminated and refused to pay the contractually triggered cancellation fee.

## 2. LIQUIDATED DAMAGES / CANCELLATION FEE — Cal. Civ. Code § 1671(b)

The cancellation fee ($150/hr × hours exceeding the prior under-contract period average) is a **liquidated damages provision** in a non-consumer commercial contract, governed by **§ 1671(b)**:

> "[A] provision in a contract liquidating the damages for the breach of the contract is valid unless the party seeking to invalidate the provision establishes that the provision was unreasonable under the circumstances existing at the time the contract was made."

**Burden is on the challenger** (Vintage Design) to prove unreasonableness as of contract formation. Key authorities:

- *Ridgley v. Topa Thrift & Loan Assn.* (1998) 17 Cal.4th 970, 977 — distinguishes valid liquidated damages from invalid penalties; the test is whether the amount represents a reasonable endeavor to estimate damages that would be difficult to ascertain.
- *Hitz v. First Interstate Bank* (1995) 38 Cal.App.4th 274, 286–289 — § 1671(b) presumption of validity; commercial parties' negotiated allocations of risk receive deference.
- *El Centro Mall, LLC v. Payless ShoeSource, Inc.* (2009) 174 Cal.App.4th 58, 63 — clause assessed at time of contracting, not in hindsight; reasonableness considers difficulty of computing actual damages.
- *Utility Consumers' Action Network, Inc. v. AT&T Broadband* (2006) 135 Cal.App.4th 1023 — courts respect bargained commercial liquidated damages.
- *Greentree Financial Group, Inc. v. Execute Sports, Inc.* (2008) 163 Cal.App.4th 495, 499–500 — **framework case**; the stipulated-judgment clause there was invalidated because the stipulated amount bore no reasonable relationship to actual damages. Distinguishable here: the $150/hour cancellation rate is tied to contemporaneous measured usage and sits at or below the Agreement's own bargained out-of-contract rates ($150/$200).

**Application:** The cancellation fee here is reasonable because:

- It is keyed to **actual measured usage** (ticketing data) — not a flat punitive sum.
- Damages from a mid-term cancellation of an MSP contract (lost margin on contracted-but-unused capacity, ramp-down costs, allocated overhead) are inherently difficult to compute, justifying liquidation.
- The $150/hour cancellation rate sits **at or below the Agreement's own "Out of Contract" rates for the same IRV-TS1 Tech Support category — $150/hr onsite normal and $200/hr onsite after-hours**. A rate that does not exceed the bargained alternative rate is, as a matter of law, not a penalty.
- The provision was specifically initialed and signed by Vintage Design's VP Finance, Erica Garcia.

## 3. OPEN BOOK ACCOUNT — Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 337a

Statutory definition:

> "The term 'book account' means a detailed statement which constitutes the principal record of one or more transactions between a debtor and a creditor . . . kept in a reasonably permanent form and manner . . . ."
> Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 337a.

**Four-year statute of limitations** on open book accounts: Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 337(b).

Leading authorities:
- *Interstate Group Administrators, Inc. v. Cravens, Dargan & Co.* (1985) 174 Cal.App.3d 700, 708 — describes open book account requirements; ledger entries with running balances suffice.
- *R.N.C. Inc. v. Tsegeletos* (1991) 231 Cal.App.3d 967, 971–972 — open book account proper where parties have ongoing series of debits/credits with running balance.
- *Robin v. Crowell* (2020) 55 Cal.App.5th 727 — accrual rules for book account claims.
- *Maggio, Inc. v. Neal* (1987) 196 Cal.App.3d 745, 752 — distinguishes book account from account stated.

**Application:** Technijian's QuickBooks/billing system is a "principal record" of the parties' transactions, with monthly invoices, payments, and running balance — squarely within § 337a.

## 4. ACCOUNT STATED

Elements:

> "The essential elements of an account stated are: (1) previous transactions between the parties establishing the relationship of debtor and creditor; (2) an agreement between the parties, express or implied, on the amount due from the debtor to the creditor; and (3) a promise by the debtor, express or implied, to pay the amount due."
> *Zinn v. Fred R. Bright Co.* (1969) 271 Cal.App.2d 597, 600.

See also:
- *Maggio, Inc. v. Neal* (1987) 196 Cal.App.3d 745, 752–753.
- *Trafton v. Youngblood* (1968) 69 Cal.2d 17, 25 — assent to amount due may be implied from retention of statement without objection within reasonable time.
- *Gleason v. Klamer* (1980) 103 Cal.App.3d 782, 787 — failure to object to invoice within reasonable time is implied admission of correctness.
- *Truestone, Inc. v. Simi West Indus. Park II* (1984) 163 Cal.App.3d 715, 725.

**Application:** Vintage Design received and paid monthly invoices for ~27 months without objection, and never objected within the 60-day contractual objection window (T&C § 3.01) to any of the support hours documented through ticketing. This establishes implied assent to the stated amounts and supports the Third Cause of Action.

## 5. SERVICES RENDERED / QUANTUM MERUIT (ALTERNATIVE)

> "[T]o recover in quantum meruit, a party need not prove the existence of a contract, but it must show the circumstances were such that the services were rendered under some understanding or expectation of both parties that compensation therefor was to be made."
> *Huskinson & Brown v. Wolf* (2004) 32 Cal.4th 453, 458.

See also:
- *Maglica v. Maglica* (1998) 66 Cal.App.4th 442 — measure of recovery is the reasonable value of services.
- *Hedging Concepts, Inc. v. First Alliance Mortg. Co.* (1996) 41 Cal.App.4th 1410, 1419.
- *Chodos v. Borman* (2014) 227 Cal.App.4th 76 — services rendered claim available where contract recovery is uncertain.

**Application:** Pleaded in the alternative (Fourth Cause of Action). Even if any portion of the contract is found unenforceable, Vintage Design received and accepted ticketed support services and owes their reasonable value. The "out-of-contract" rate ($150/hr remote tech support) is the contractual benchmark for reasonable value.

## 6. LATE FEES AS LIQUIDATED DAMAGES

10% late fee enforceable under § 1671(b) commercial standard:

- *Garrett v. Coast & Southern Fed. Sav. & Loan Assn.* (1973) 9 Cal.3d 731, 738–740 — **framework case**; invalidated the 2%/month consumer-loan late charge as a penalty unrelated to actual harm. Distinguishable here: (a) B2B commercial MSP contract, not consumer lending; (b) the 10% charge applies only after a 10-day grace period; (c) charge is proportionate to documented cost of capital and collection effort.
- *Beasley v. Wells Fargo Bank* (1991) 235 Cal.App.3d 1383 — **framework case**; struck down specific bank NSF/late-fee schedule as penalties under *Garrett*. Distinguishable on same grounds as *Garrett*; also a consumer-deposit-account context, not commercial.
- *Hitz v. First Interstate Bank, supra*, 38 Cal.App.4th at 289 — affirmed enforcement of a liquidated-damages measure tied to actual harm; confirms § 1671(b) presumption of validity applies in commercial settings.

**Application:** The 10% late fee was disclosed both in the Agreement (Other Terms ¶ 3) and on every invoice; it is keyed to actual unpaid amounts and reflects collection costs and lost time-value of money. Reasonable under § 1671(b).

## 7. AUTHORITY OF CORPORATE OFFICER TO BIND

Vintage Design's VP Finance Erica Garcia signed the 5/4/2023 Agreement. Anticipated defense ("she lacked authority") fails on apparent authority + ratification grounds:

- *Pasadena Medi-Center Associates v. Superior Court* (1973) 9 Cal.3d 773, 779–780 — officer holding a title that conveys general authority over the subject matter (here, financial obligations) has apparent authority to bind the corporation; ratification arises through acceptance of contract benefits.
- *Memorial Hospitals Assn. v. Randol* (1995) 38 Cal.App.4th 1300, 1308 — apparent authority of officer holding title that suggests management responsibility.
- *Lippert v. Bailey* (1966) 241 Cal.App.2d 376, 382 — apparent authority binding where principal cloaks agent with indicia of authority.
- *Rakestraw v. Rodrigues* (1972) 8 Cal.3d 67, 73 — ratification by acceptance of contract benefits with knowledge of the material terms.

**Note on Cal. Corp. Code § 313.** Section 313's "conclusive presumption" for third parties turns on a signature by a specified combination of officers (a chair/president/VP **plus** a secretary, assistant secretary, CFO, or assistant treasurer). *Snukal v. Flightways Mfg., Inc.* (2000) 23 Cal.4th 754, 782, found the presumption on that two-officer pairing. A single VP Finance signature, standing alone, does not trigger § 313's statutory presumption. Technijian's authority argument therefore rests on apparent authority and ratification, not § 313 — arguments which are amply supported on the 27-month course of performance.

**Application:** Erica Garcia identified herself as VP Finance (a title conveying general authority over the corporation's financial obligations), signed via DocuSign on Vintage Design's behalf, and Vintage Design thereafter accepted services and paid monthly invoices for ~27 months without objection. Apparent authority is established; ratification is established by 27 months of paid performance.

## 8. COURSE OF DEALING, ACCEPTANCE, AND MODIFICATION

The Agreement (T&C § 1.06) provides that if Client does not object in writing within 30 days of any modification, Client is bound. Course-of-dealing principles reinforce:

- Cal. Com. Code § 1303 (course of performance, course of dealing).
- *Wagner v. Glendale Adventist Med. Ctr.* (1989) 216 Cal.App.3d 1379, 1388 — long course of performance establishes parties' interpretation of contract.
- *Employers Reinsurance Co. v. Superior Court* (2008) 161 Cal.App.4th 906, 921 — practical construction of contract by parties' conduct is highly persuasive.
- *Crestview Cemetery Assn. v. Dieden* (1960) 54 Cal.2d 744, 753 — silence with knowledge can constitute acceptance.

**Application:** Vintage Design's payment of every monthly invoice for 27 months — invoices that explicitly attached the Agreement terms (per Demand ¶ 16) and itemized contracted hours, ticketed hours, and unpaid amounts — establishes both acceptance and a course-of-dealing interpretation favorable to Technijian.

## 9. DUAL WAIVER OF RIGHT TO OBJECT — WEEKLY (30-DAY) + MONTHLY (60-DAY)

Technijian's billing practice provided Vintage Design with **two separate, independently sufficient opportunities** to dispute every hour of work performed:

### A. Weekly Zero-Dollar Invoices with 30-Day Dispute Window

Throughout the 27-month performance period, Technijian transmitted **weekly zero-dollar invoices** to Vintage Design enumerating every ticket, time entry, technician, start/end timestamps, and hours worked that week. Each weekly notice afforded Vintage Design a **30-day window to dispute any work performed**. Approximately **140 such weekly notices** were issued. Vintage Design disputed **none**.

### B. Monthly Invoices with 60-Day Objection Window (T&C § 3.01)

T&C § 3.01: *"Client shall have sixty (60) days from the receipt of any invoice or bill to notify Technijian of any errors . . . If Client does not notify Technijian within the sixty (60) day time period, Client shall be deemed to have accepted without question such invoice or bill . . . ."*

Approximately 27 monthly invoices across the relationship, each attaching the Agreement terms and noting that "hours not paid are due if support agreement is cancelled." Vintage Design disputed **none**.

### Authorities — both waivers are enforceable

- *Cobb v. Pacific Mut. Life Ins. Co.* (1935) 4 Cal.2d 565, 573 — contractual time limits on objections enforced; failure to dispute within the contractually specified period forecloses later challenge.
- *Gleason v. Klamer* (1980) 103 Cal.App.3d 782, 787 — failure to object to invoice within reasonable time is implied admission of correctness.
- *Trafton v. Youngblood* (1968) 69 Cal.2d 17, 25 — assent to amount due may be implied from retention of statement without objection within reasonable time.
- *Pollard v. Saxe & Yolles Dev. Co.* (1974) 12 Cal.3d 374, 380 — purpose of notice provisions is to allow seller to investigate and cure while evidence is fresh.
- **Cal. Com. Code § 1303 (course of performance)** — repeated conduct by both parties without objection establishes the parties' interpretation and acceptance. *Wagner v. Glendale Adventist Med. Ctr.* (1989) 216 Cal.App.3d 1379, 1388.
- **Cal. Com. Code § 2607(3)(a)** (by analogy from goods) — buyer must notify seller of breach within reasonable time after discovery or be barred.
- *Tatum v. Schering Corp.* (S.D. Cal. 1991) — contractual time-bar on disputing invoices enforced in commercial context.
- *Employers Reinsurance Co. v. Superior Court* (2008) 161 Cal.App.4th 906, 921 — practical construction of contract by parties' conduct is highly persuasive.

### Application

Under the **weekly 30-day waiver alone**, Vintage Design is barred from disputing any ticketed hour it did not challenge during a 30-day window across ~140 weekly notices over 27 months. Under the **monthly 60-day waiver alone (T&C § 3.01)**, Vintage Design is barred from disputing any billed hour it did not challenge within 60 days of each monthly invoice. Applied together, **every ticketed hour underlying Technijian's cancellation-fee calculation is effectively indisputable as a matter of contract.** Respondent's "the records aren't accurate" or "we didn't actually use those hours" arguments are foreclosed at the threshold, before the merits of the cancellation clause are even reached.

This is also the single strongest evidentiary foundation for the business-records admissibility argument under **Cal. Evid. Code §§ 1271, 1552, 1553** (Issue 23 below): contemporaneous records that the client had a weekly and monthly opportunity to audit and dispute — and did not — are as trustworthy as commercial records get.

## 10. CONTINUED PERFORMANCE AFTER TERMINATION NOTICE — NOT A WAIVER

Anticipated defense: Technijian continued invoicing/serving Vintage Design after the alleged 7/31/2025 termination, so termination is "in dispute" or Technijian "waived" cancellation fee. Defense fails:

- *DRG/Beverly Hills, Ltd. v. Chopstix Dim Sum Cafe & Takeout III, Ltd.* (1994) 30 Cal.App.4th 54, 60 — "Waiver requires the voluntary act of relinquishing a known right." Mere continued performance does not effect waiver absent clear intent.
- *Salyer Grain & Milling Co. v. Henson* (1970) 13 Cal.App.3d 493, 501 — waiver of contract rights requires clear and convincing evidence of intent.
- *Wind Dancer Production Group v. Walt Disney Pictures* (2017) 10 Cal.App.5th 56, 78 — accepting partial performance does not waive right to insist on full performance.
- *Old Republic Ins. Co. v. FSR Brokerage, Inc.* (2000) 80 Cal.App.4th 666, 678 — waiver is fact-intensive; defendant bears burden.

**Application:** The defense has no factual footing. Technijian performed no services for Vintage Design after the July 31, 2025 effective termination date; there are no post-termination invoices, tickets, or engagements in the record. Technijian's assessment of the cancellation fee on June 27, 2025 is the controlling notice of intent, and the absence of any post-termination activity is itself affirmative evidence that Technijian treated the relationship as terminated.

## 11. ATTORNEYS' FEES — T&C § 5.02 Indemnity + Cal. Civ. Code § 1717 Reciprocity

The Agreement's fee-recovery hook is T&C § 5.02, which provides that Client "agrees to indemnify, defend, and hold harmless Technijian . . . from and against any loss, liability, damage, penalty or expense (**including attorneys' fees, expert witness fees and cost of defense**) they may suffer or incur as a result of (i) **any failure by Client or any employee, agent or affiliate of Client to comply with the terms of this Agreement**." Vintage Design's non-payment of the cancellation fee is precisely such a failure.

Cal. Civ. Code § 1717 renders the indemnity reciprocal in favor of the prevailing party on any contract claim, even where the contractual clause is drafted as one-way. Key authorities:

- *Santisas v. Goodin* (1998) 17 Cal.4th 599, 610–611 — § 1717 applies to make fee provisions mutual; prevailing party recovers fees on contract claims.
- *Hsu v. Abbara* (1995) 9 Cal.4th 863, 871 — definition of "prevailing party" for fee shifting.
- *Trope v. Katz* (1995) 11 Cal.4th 274 — fees recoverable for contract enforcement.
- *PLCM Group, Inc. v. Drexler* (2000) 22 Cal.4th 1084, 1095 — lodestar method; fees include those incurred in arbitration.
- *Cassel v. Superior Court* (2011) 51 Cal.4th 113 — confidentiality of mediation does not affect entitlement to fees.
- *Carole Ring & Associates v. Nicastro* (2001) 87 Cal.App.4th 253 — fees recoverable through arbitration award.

**Application:** Technijian, on prevailing, will recover all reasonable attorneys' fees and arbitration costs. This dramatically increases Vintage Design's exposure if the matter proceeds to award.

## 12. PREJUDGMENT INTEREST

Two statutory bases:

**Cal. Civ. Code § 3287(a)** — interest as of right where damages are certain or capable of being made certain by calculation as of a particular day:

- *Wisper Corp. v. California Commerce Bank* (1996) 49 Cal.App.4th 948, 958–960 — § 3287(a) interest available where amount is liquidated or readily ascertainable.
- *Howard v. American National Fire Ins. Co.* (2010) 187 Cal.App.4th 498, 535 — interest accrues from date amount became certain.
- *Children's Hospital & Medical Center v. Bontá* (2002) 97 Cal.App.4th 740, 774 — calculation-from-records satisfies certainty requirement.

**Cal. Civ. Code § 3289(b)** — 10% per annum on contract claims absent specified rate:

- *North Oakland Med. Clinic v. Rogers* (1998) 65 Cal.App.4th 824, 829–830 — § 3289(b) imposes 10% legal rate on breach of contract damages.
- *Continental Heller Corp. v. Amtech Mech. Services* (1997) 53 Cal.App.4th 500, 511 — applies in commercial settings.

**Application:** The cancellation fee ($240,487.50 contract-correct, as the Demand will be conformed; or $219,575.40 under the spreadsheet-net-excess reading) is fully ascertainable from Technijian's ticketing system and contracted rate; § 3287(a) interest applies. Absent a higher contractual rate, § 3289(b) supplies 10% per annum running from July 27, 2025.

## 13. AAA COMMERCIAL ARBITRATION — ENFORCEABILITY AND SCOPE

T&C § 6.10 mandates AAA Commercial Arbitration:

- *AT&T Mobility LLC v. Concepcion* (2011) 563 U.S. 333 — FAA preempts state-law obstacles to arbitration enforcement.
- *Pinnacle Museum Tower Assn. v. Pinnacle Market Development* (2012) 55 Cal.4th 223 — commercial arbitration agreements presumptively valid.
- *Moncharsh v. Heily & Blase* (1992) 3 Cal.4th 1 — arbitration awards reviewed only on narrow grounds; merits errors not reviewable.
- *Aerojet-General Corp. v. Transport Indemnity Insurance* (1997) 17 Cal.4th 38 — broad arbitration clauses encompass all disputes "arising from or relating to" the agreement.
- *Sandquist v. Lebo Automotive, Inc.* (2016) 1 Cal.5th 233 — on the language of this class of contracts, arbitrability questions (there, class arbitration) are for the arbitrator to decide; useful corollary that gateway issues of contract scope go to the arbitrator under the parties' chosen AAA rules.
- *Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 1286* — confirmation of arbitration award.
- *Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 1286.2* — limited grounds for vacatur.

**Application:** The arbitration clause is broad ("any claim . . . arising from or relating in any way to this Agreement or to our relationship"). All four causes of action — and any defenses Vintage Design may assert — fall within arbitral jurisdiction. Once awarded, confirmation is largely ministerial under § 1286.

## 14. PLEADING AMENDMENT / RELATION BACK / MISNOMER

## 15. UNCONSCIONABILITY — ANTICIPATED DEFENSE

If Vintage Design attacks any provision (cancellation fee, late fee, fee-shifting, arbitration) as unconscionable, the framework is:

- *Armendariz v. Foundation Health Psychcare Servs., Inc.* (2000) 24 Cal.4th 83, 113–114 — both procedural and substantive unconscionability required, on a sliding scale.
- *Sanchez v. Valencia Holding Co., LLC* (2015) 61 Cal.4th 899, 910–911 — substantive unconscionability requires terms that "shock the conscience"; merely one-sided is insufficient.
- *Pinnacle Museum Tower Assn., supra*, 55 Cal.4th at 246–247 — sophisticated commercial parties face high bar for procedural unconscionability.
- *Baltazar v. Forever 21, Inc.* (2016) 62 Cal.4th 1237, 1244 — adhesive nature alone is not enough.

**Application:** This is a B2B contract between two sophisticated entities. Vintage Design had every opportunity to negotiate, reject, or seek alternatives. The cancellation fee is rationally tied to actual measured usage. There is no procedural unconscionability (no oppression, no surprise; terms initialed) and no substantive unconscionability (terms commercially reasonable in MSP industry).

## 17. ELECTRONIC SIGNATURES / DOCUSIGN VALIDITY (UETA)

The operative Agreement is DocuSigned (Envelope ID B679C550-E41C-4E31-BE34-7F8FFF437C3D, executed 5/4/2023). California's Uniform Electronic Transactions Act controls:

- **Cal. Civ. Code § 1633.7(a)** — "A record or signature may not be denied legal effect or enforceability solely because it is in electronic form."
- **Cal. Civ. Code § 1633.7(d)** — "If a law requires a signature, an electronic signature satisfies the law."
- **Cal. Civ. Code § 1633.9(a)** — an electronic signature is attributable to a person if it was the act of the person, shown by any relevant circumstances.
- *J.B.B. Investment Partners, Ltd. v. Fair* (2014) 232 Cal.App.4th 974 — electronic signatures (including email-typed names) satisfy statute of frauds and form binding agreements when the signer intended to authenticate the writing.
- *Ruiz v. Moss Bros. Auto Group, Inc.* (2014) 232 Cal.App.4th 836, 844 — **sets the authentication standard**: the proponent of an electronic signature must offer evidence sufficient to attribute the signature to the signer; on the record in *Ruiz* (a one-sentence declaration asserting attribution), the showing was held insufficient. Technijian's record exceeds that standard: custodian declaration, full completion certificate with IP addresses and timestamps, email delivery to Erica Garcia's Vintage Design address, and 27 months of paid performance under the signed Agreement.
- *Fabian v. Renovate America, Inc.* (2019) 42 Cal.App.5th 1062, 1066–1069 — applies the *Ruiz* authentication standard in the DocuSign context; affirmed denial of motion to compel where the proponent submitted only a bare certificate without custodian testimony or linking evidence. Technijian's record meets the *Fabian* foundation requirements (metadata + custodian + corroborating performance).
- 15 U.S.C. § 7001(a) (federal E-SIGN Act) — additional federal backstop.

**Application:** The DocuSign Certificate of Completion for Envelope B679C550-E41C-4E31-BE34-7F8FFF437C3D (5/4/2023), combined with custodian declaration and 27 months of paid performance, satisfies the authentication foundation required by *Ruiz* and *Fabian* and establishes attribution under Cal. Civ. Code § 1633.9(a). Any attack on signature validity should be defeated at the foundation stage — but declarant testimony from Technijian's custodian of records should be prepared in advance to avoid the thin-record problem that tripped up the proponents in *Ruiz* and *Fabian*.

## 18. PAROL EVIDENCE RULE & INTEGRATION CLAUSE

T&C § 6.08 is a **full integration clause**: *"This Agreement . . . sets forth the entire agreement and understanding of the parties hereto . . . and supersedes all prior agreements, promises, covenants, arrangements, communications, representations, or warranties, whether oral or written . . . ."*

- **Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 1856(a)** — "Terms set forth in a writing intended by the parties as a final expression of their agreement . . . may not be contradicted by evidence of a prior agreement or of a contemporaneous oral agreement."
- *Casa Herrera, Inc. v. Beydoun* (2004) 32 Cal.4th 336, 343–344 — parol evidence rule is a rule of substantive law barring consideration of extrinsic evidence to vary integrated agreements.
- *Banco do Brasil, S.A. v. Latian, Inc.* (1991) 234 Cal.App.3d 973, 1001–1002 — strong presumption of integration where contract contains explicit integration clause.
- *EPA Real Estate Partnership v. Kang* (1992) 12 Cal.App.4th 171, 175–176 — integration clause conclusive absent fraud or mutual mistake.
- *Riverisland Cold Storage, Inc. v. Fresno-Madera Production Credit Assn.* (2013) 55 Cal.4th 1169, 1172 — overruled *Bank of America etc. Assn. v. Pendergrass* (1935) 4 Cal.2d 258 and broadened the fraud exception to the parol evidence rule. Extrinsic evidence of fraud is now admissible even where it directly varies the integrated terms; but the fraud exception still requires a pleading and proof of each element of fraud, and does not reach promissory fraud inconsistent with a written term where the party was represented and sophisticated at contracting.

**Application:** Any attempt by Vintage Design to introduce side representations (alleged oral assurances of lower termination fees, promises of rate discounts, etc.) is barred by the integration clause. Technijian should invoke the parol evidence rule at the earliest procedural opportunity.

## 19. CONTRACT INTERPRETATION — PLAIN MEANING AND CONSTRUCTION RULES

The cancellation fee clause is unambiguous. Standard California interpretation rules apply:

- **Cal. Civ. Code § 1636** — contract construed to give effect to the mutual intent of the parties.
- **Cal. Civ. Code § 1638** — "clear and explicit" language governs if it does not involve absurdity.
- **Cal. Civ. Code § 1639** — "[W]hen a contract is reduced to writing, the intention of the parties is to be ascertained from the writing alone, if possible."
- **Cal. Civ. Code § 1641** — "The whole of a contract is to be taken together, so as to give effect to every part, if reasonably practicable."
- *Pacific Gas & E. Co. v. G. W. Thomas Drayage & Rigging Co.* (1968) 69 Cal.2d 33 — extrinsic evidence admissible to show ambiguity but must be reasonably susceptible.
- *City of Atascadero v. Merrill Lynch* (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 445, 473 — unambiguous terms enforced as written.
- *Powerine Oil Co. v. Superior Court* (2005) 37 Cal.4th 377, 390–391 — contract interpreted to give effect to every provision.
- *MacKinnon v. Truck Ins. Exchange* (2003) 31 Cal.4th 635, 648 — reasonable expectations of the parties control.
- **Cal. Civ. Code § 1654** (ambiguity construed against drafter) — to be anticipated; Technijian's response is that the cancellation fee clause is plain, not ambiguous.

**Application:** The cancellation fee clause expressly defines the trigger (termination), the measure (excess hours documented through ticketing), and the rate ($150/hour). No ambiguity; § 1638 controls. If Vintage Design forces an ambiguity fight, trade-usage evidence from the MSP industry reinforces Technijian's reading.

## 20. GENERAL DAMAGES FOR BREACH AND CERTAINTY OF DAMAGES

- **Cal. Civ. Code § 3300** — "For the breach of an obligation arising from contract, the measure of damages . . . is the amount which will compensate the party aggrieved for all the detriment proximately caused thereby, or which, in the ordinary course of things, would be likely to result therefrom."
- **Cal. Civ. Code § 3301** — damages must be "clearly ascertainable in both their nature and origin."
- *Lewis Jorge Construction Mgmt., Inc. v. Pomona Unified School Dist.* (2004) 34 Cal.4th 960, 968–970 — general vs. special damages; foreseeability.
- *Sargon Enterprises, Inc. v. University of Southern California* (2012) 55 Cal.4th 747, 773–775 — damages require reasonable certainty, not mathematical precision; burden is on plaintiff but liberally applied.
- *Vestar Development II, LLC v. General Dynamics Corp.* (9th Cir. 2001) 249 F.3d 958, 961–962 — commercial damages need only be proved with reasonable certainty.
- *Toscano v. Greene Music* (2004) 124 Cal.App.4th 685, 694 — liquidated damages provisions remove certainty as an issue.

**Application:** Because the cancellation fee is a liquidated damages provision tied to actual ticketed hours at a stipulated rate, the "certainty" burden is dramatically reduced. The arbitrator need only confirm the number of hours and apply the stipulated $150/hour multiplier. *Toscano* directly supports this.

## 22. ANTICIPATORY BREACH / REPUDIATION / TOTAL BREACH

Vintage Design's refusal to pay the cancellation fee (and continued refusal through the arbitration demand) constitutes repudiation, supporting immediate and complete recovery:

- *Taylor v. Johnston* (1975) 15 Cal.3d 130, 137 — anticipatory breach occurs when one party, prior to time of performance, declares intent not to perform; entitles non-breaching party to sue immediately.
- *Central Valley General Hospital v. Smith* (2008) 162 Cal.App.4th 501, 514–515 — distinguishes partial breach from total breach; refusal to pay a liquidated obligation is total breach.
- *Romano v. Rockwell Internat., Inc.* (1996) 14 Cal.4th 479, 488–490 — anticipatory breach doctrine permits acceleration of all contractual obligations.
- *Brown v. Grimes* (2011) 192 Cal.App.4th 265, 277 — total breach excuses further performance by non-breaching party and entitles full damages.

**Application:** Vintage Design's (a) termination notice, (b) failure to pay the final invoice by July 27, 2025, and (c) failure to respond to pre-arbitration demand, constitute total breach/repudiation. Technijian is entitled to the full accelerated cancellation fee without waiting out any remaining performance obligation.

## 23. EVIDENCE — ADMISSIBILITY OF TICKETING RECORDS AND INVOICES

The case turns heavily on Technijian's contemporaneous ticketing and invoicing records. California evidence rules admit them:

- **Cal. Evid. Code § 1271 (Business Records Exception)** — records of regularly conducted business activity admissible where: (a) made in regular course of business, (b) at or near the time of the event, (c) by or from information transmitted by persons with knowledge, and (d) trustworthy.
- **Cal. Evid. Code § 1552** — printed representations of computer information presumed accurate.
- **Cal. Evid. Code § 1553** — similar presumption for digitally stored images and records.
- *People v. Hernandez* (1997) 55 Cal.App.4th 225, 240 — QuickBooks and electronic ledger systems qualify as business records.
- *Jazayeri v. Mao* (2009) 174 Cal.App.4th 301, 324 — computer-generated business records admissible with foundational testimony from custodian.
- *Aguimatang v. California State Lottery* (1991) 234 Cal.App.3d 769, 797–798 — reliability of electronic records presumed where inputs are regular and verifiable.
- AAA Commercial Rule R-34 — arbitrator determines admissibility; business records routinely admitted.

**Application:** Technijian's ticketing extracts (timestamped tickets, assignee, duration, description), monthly invoices, and QuickBooks ledger are all admissible as business records. Foundation will be laid through a declaration/testimony from Technijian's Controller or systems administrator. Vintage Design's burden to rebut trustworthiness is high and unsupported on this record.

## 24. RATIFICATION BY ACCEPTANCE OF BENEFITS

Even if Vintage Design challenged the authority of any signer or the validity of any amendment, **ratification** cures:

- *Rakestraw v. Rodrigues* (1972) 8 Cal.3d 67, 73 — acceptance of benefits with knowledge constitutes ratification.
- *Reusche v. California Pacific Title Ins. Co.* (1965) 231 Cal.App.2d 731, 737 — ratification requires: (1) principal's knowledge of material facts; (2) act manifesting intent to ratify.
- *Pasadena Medi-Center Associates v. Superior Court* (1973) 9 Cal.3d 773, 779–780 — principal bound by unauthorized contract where benefits accepted after discovery of facts.
- *Common Wealth Ins. Systems, Inc. v. Kersten* (1974) 40 Cal.App.3d 1014, 1026 — ratification may be implied from conduct inconsistent with repudiation.

**Application:** For 27 months, Vintage Design received Technijian's services, paid monthly invoices that attached the Agreement terms and referenced the cancellation fee, and objected to none of it. Even if (counterfactually) an authority defect existed, ratification is established as a matter of law.

## 26. IMPLIED COVENANT OF GOOD FAITH AND FAIR DEALING

- *Guz v. Bechtel National, Inc.* (2000) 24 Cal.4th 317, 349 — every contract contains an implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing; tort damages generally unavailable in ordinary commercial context.
- *Carma Developers (Cal.), Inc. v. Marathon Development California, Inc.* (1992) 2 Cal.4th 342, 371–374 — implied covenant does not override express terms but prohibits frustrating the other party's reasonable expectations.

**Application (offensive & defensive):**

- *Defensive* — The premise of any "post-termination invoicing" theory is absent from the record; Technijian performed no services for Vintage Design after the July 31, 2025 effective termination. There are no post-termination invoices, tickets, or engagements to characterize one way or another.

## 30. POST-JUDGMENT INTEREST ON CONFIRMED ARBITRATION AWARD
