## SCENARIOS BELOW THE $125,000 FLOOR — WHY THEY ARE NOT LEGALLY PLAUSIBLE

The following three scenarios are the readings Respondent's counsel is expected to advance. Each is defeated by controlling California authority and the agreement's own text.

### Scenario E1 — Only P3 (Terminated Period) Excess × $150

**Respondent's argument:** The cancellation clause in ¶ 5 only reaches the period during which termination occurs. Because P3 (May–Jul 2025) ran *below* billed by 239.31 hours, no cancellation fee is owed. **Asserted total: $0.**

**Why this reading fails:**

1. **Clause text defeats it.** Under Contract ¶ 5 provides that "**any hours that exceeded the previous under contract period average, that were documented through ticketing**" are subject to the fee. The language is cumulative and unqualified — it is not limited to "any hours in the current period." If the drafters had meant to limit the clause to the terminated period, they would have said so. Cal. Civ. Code § 1638; *City of Atascadero v. Merrill Lynch* (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 445, 473.

2. **The whole-contract construction rule defeats it.** Reading ¶ 5 to reach only the terminated period would render the cancellation fee a dead letter in virtually every real-world case, because clients rarely terminate mid-spike — they terminate after the spike has subsided, or at the start of a new period before significant hours accrue. A reading that renders the clause meaningless violates Cal. Civ. Code § 1641 (whole contract construed to give effect to every part) and *Powerine Oil Co. v. Superior Court* (2005) 37 Cal.4th 377, 390–391.

3. **¶¶ 3–4 are prospective, not retroactive.** The averaging mechanism in ¶¶ 3–4 adjusts **future** monthly billing at the end of each completed period. It does not **forgive** unpaid excess from prior periods — it simply raises the baseline going forward. Technijian's P2 and P3 monthly billing did in fact step up (80/mo → ~170/mo → ~210/mo) reflecting this adjustment. But the P1 excess hours (1,010.97 hrs) were already delivered and never paid for; the prospective averaging does not compensate for them. The only contractual mechanism that does compensate Technijian for that delivered-but-unpaid excess is the cancellation fee in ¶ 5.

4. **Two independent dispute waivers bar any "we didn't owe for those hours" argument.**
   - **Weekly 30-day dispute waiver.** Throughout the ~27-month performance period, Technijian sent Vintage Design **weekly zero-dollar invoices** — approximately **140 of them** — each enumerating every ticket, time entry, technician assignment, and hours worked that week, and each expressly providing Vintage Design with a 30-day window to dispute any work performed. Vintage Design disputed **none**. Under contract course-of-performance principles, this is dispositive acceptance of the ticketed hours. Cal. Com. Code § 1303; *Wagner v. Glendale Adventist Med. Ctr.* (1989) 216 Cal.App.3d 1379, 1388.
   - **Monthly 60-day objection waiver.** T&C § 3.01 provides: "If Client does not notify Technijian within the sixty (60) day[] period, Client shall be deemed to have accepted without question such invoice or bill." Vintage Design never objected to any monthly invoice. Every invoice itemized "Hours not paid are due if support agreement is cancelled per monthly service agreement." *Cobb v. Pacific Mut. Life Ins. Co.* (1935) 4 Cal.2d 565, 573; *Gleason v. Klamer* (1980) 103 Cal.App.3d 782, 787 (failure to object within reasonable time is implied admission of correctness).

   Either waiver alone bars any attack on ticket accuracy; both combined make the hours effectively indisputable under any theory.

5. **Ratification and course of performance cement the reading.** Erica Garcia, as Vintage Design's VP Finance, personally reviewed and paid each monthly invoice for over two years. *Rakestraw v. Rodrigues* (1972) 8 Cal.3d 67, 73 (ratification by acceptance of benefits with knowledge); *Wagner v. Glendale Adventist Med. Ctr.* (1989) 216 Cal.App.3d 1379, 1388 (long course of performance persuasive as to parties' interpretation).

**Bottom line on E1:** Treating only P3's excess rewrites the cancellation clause. No California authority supports it. It is not a plausible outcome.

### Scenario E2 — Only P2 + P3 Excess × $150 (P1 "Trued Up")

**Respondent's argument:** P1 was "trued up" at end of period under ¶¶ 3–4; the averaging mechanism converted P1's excess into a higher P2 billing baseline. Therefore P1's 1,010.97 excess hours are forever forgiven, and the cancellation fee reaches only P2 (559.10) + P3 (−239.31) = **319.79 hours × $150 = $52,765.35 including late fee**.

**Why this reading fails:**

1. **Same whole-contract infirmity as E1.** The cancellation clause reads on "**any hours that exceeded the previous under contract period average**" — not "any hours in the current or most recent period only." If P1 hours "exceeded" that period's average (they did: billed 960, actual 1,970.97 — more than double), they remain within the textual reach of ¶ 5 at termination. Cal. Civ. Code §§ 1638, 1641.

2. **No provision in ¶¶ 3–4 states that completed-period excess is released.** Paragraph 3 states: "The first month of the new average will be charged at the previous average since that invoice will be due the first of the month." Paragraph 4 states: "If the average goes down a credit will be given on the next month's invoice. If the average goes up an extra charge will be given on the next month's invoice." **Neither paragraph uses the word "release," "forgive," "credit back," or any other term of absolution.** They describe billing adjustments going forward. Courts will not imply a release that the parties did not write. *Series AGI West Linn of Appian Group Investors DE v. Eves* (2013) 217 Cal.App.4th 156, 164–165.

3. **The averaging mechanism does not compensate Technijian for the delivered excess.** A higher P2 billed baseline charges Vintage Design prospectively for the ongoing elevated demand pattern. It does not pay Technijian retroactively for the P1 hours that already walked out the door. Technijian billed 960 hours in P1 and delivered 1,970.97 — the 1,010.97 hour gap was never invoiced at the averaged rate; it was deferred to the cancellation-fee trigger in ¶ 5. To eliminate P1 from the cancellation calculation is to eliminate the whole economic purpose of the clause.

4. **California disfavors reading commercial clauses to be meaningless.** *MacKinnon v. Truck Ins. Exchange* (2003) 31 Cal.4th 635, 648 (reasonable expectations of the parties control); *Powerine Oil*, supra. No reasonable party in Technijian's position would have agreed to a cancellation clause that could be defeated by the simple expedient of terminating after each period closed — which is exactly what E2 proposes.

5. **§ 1671(b) burden still on Respondent.** Even if this reading were textually arguable (it is not), Respondent would bear the burden under Cal. Civ. Code § 1671(b) of proving the clause's application to P1 is "unreasonable under the circumstances existing at the time the contract was made." *Ridgley v. Topa Thrift & Loan Assn.* (1998) 17 Cal.4th 970, 977. That showing cannot be made — the clause was disclosed on every invoice for 27 months, specifically authorized charging at $150/hr for excess ticketed hours, and Respondent made no objection throughout performance.

**Bottom line on E2:** The P1-was-trued-up argument has intuitive appeal but no textual hook. The drafted averaging mechanism is prospective by its own words. Rejecting E2 is a straightforward application of *Powerine Oil*, § 1641, and § 1671(b).

### Scenario C — Native Rates Knockdown ($20,013.73)

**Respondent's argument:** The $150/hr cancellation rate is a penalty disguised as liquidated damages. It should be reduced to each category's native contract rate — $15 offshore normal, $30 offshore AH, $125 onshore normal. Net dollar computation: **$18,194.30 + 10% late fee = $20,013.73**.

**Why this reading fails:**

1. **§ 1671(b) squarely forecloses it.** The statute provides that "**a provision in a contract liquidating the damages for the breach of the contract is valid unless the party seeking to invalidate the provision establishes that the provision was unreasonable under the circumstances existing at the time the contract was made**." The burden is on Respondent. *Ridgley v. Topa Thrift*, supra, 17 Cal.4th at 977; *Hitz v. First Interstate Bank* (1995) 38 Cal.App.4th 274, 286–289.

2. **The $150 rate is demonstrably not punitive.** The Agreement's own "Out of Contract" rate table for the same IRV-TS1 Tech Support role is **$150/hr onsite normal and $200/hr onsite after-hours**. The cancellation rate is exactly at or below what Vintage Design would pay for the same service outside of contract. A rate that does not exceed the bargained alternative rate is, as a matter of law, not a penalty. *El Centro Mall, LLC v. Payless ShoeSource, Inc.* (2009) 174 Cal.App.4th 58, 63; *Greentree Financial Group, Inc. v. Execute Sports, Inc.* (2008) 163 Cal.App.4th 495.

3. **Reasonableness is assessed at formation, not in hindsight.** *El Centro Mall*, supra, 174 Cal.App.4th at 63. At contract formation in May 2023, the cancellation rate was fully disclosed, specifically initialed by Erica Garcia, and clearly tied to **actual measured usage** (hours documented through ticketing). These are the classic indicators of a reasonable liquidation of damages that would otherwise be difficult to compute — ramp-up costs, dedicated staff capacity, allocated overhead. *Ridgley*, supra, 17 Cal.4th at 977.

4. **No procedural unconscionability.** This is a B2B contract between two sophisticated entities. Vintage Design had a VP Finance sign it — a professional with explicit fiduciary responsibility over the company's contractual obligations. *Pinnacle Museum Tower Assn. v. Pinnacle Market Development* (2012) 55 Cal.4th 223, 246–247 (sophisticated commercial parties face a high bar); *Sanchez v. Valencia Holding Co., LLC* (2015) 61 Cal.4th 899, 910–911 (substantive unconscionability requires terms that "shock the conscience").

5. **Economic substance supports the rate.** Technijian scaled its offshore and onshore support pods to match Vintage Design's actual demand, which ran substantially above the contracted baseline for most of the ~27 months. Termination mid-Period 3 leaves Technijian holding dedicated scaled capacity with no ability to redeploy at the pace of ramp-up. The $150 cancellation rate is the contractually bargained compensation for that sunk operational investment — reasonable under any view of commercial MSP norms.

6. **Native-rate knockdown would reward delay.** If Respondent could delay payment of an invoiced cancellation fee long enough to force arbitration, and then recover to native rates, every client would do so. *Hitz*, supra, 38 Cal.App.4th at 289 (rejecting after-the-fact rate reduction attempts).

**Bottom line on C:** Respondent cannot meet the § 1671(b) burden. The $150 rate is below the out-of-contract alternative, tied to measured usage, disclosed, initialed, and economically grounded. There is no California case granting a penalty-knockdown on similar facts in the commercial MSP context.

### Expected-value justification for $125,000 floor

Based on probability-weighting across scenarios:

- 75% cumulative probability of a $200K+ recovery outcome (Scenarios Demand, B, A)
- 10% probability of aggressive Scenario D outcome (~$310K)
- 15% probability of reduced Scenarios C/E2 outcomes ($20K–$53K)

Probability-weighted expected recovery at award **≈ $205K gross**, less combined arbitration costs and fees-to-award (~$80K–$135K) that are partially shiftable under T&C § 5.02 + § 1717.

Settling at $125K yields **certainty of ~$65K net after fees-to-date**, avoids the 15% downside risk, and avoids 6+ months of arbitration and deposition costs. Below $125K, arbitrating to award becomes the better economic bet.
