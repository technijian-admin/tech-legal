## Statutory / Regulatory Framework

### Bus. & Prof. Code § 7169 (AB 1070, Stats. 2017, ch. 662)
Requires CSLB, in collaboration with CPUC, to develop the **Solar Energy System Disclosure Document** for consumer disclosure on every residential solar contract. Effective Jan 1, 2019. Failure to provide = unlawful business practice = UCL § 17200 predicate.

### CPUC Solar Consumer Protection Guide (current rev. 2022)
Mandatory delivery before interconnection. Requires plain-language disclosure of system performance expectations. Sunrun's vague "system issue" notices arguably violate the *spirit* of the disclosure regime — consistent disclosure obligation continues post-installation when the provider holds production data.

### CSLB License Conditions (Class C-46 Solar)
A C-46 contractor must complete work in a "good and workmanlike manner" (Bus. & Prof. § 7110). Production failure plus 67-day silence + multiple no-fix appointments arguably violates this. CSLB enforcement precedent:

- **CSLB Accusation v. SunPower Corporate Systems** (filed March 28, 2024) — CSLB sought license revocation for failure to repair / declining to address documented installation defects. Hearing scheduled April 14, 2025. Demonstrates CSLB will act on solar contractor non-response.
- **People v. Vivint Solar** (Riv. DA 2021; SD DA Feb 2026) — multi-county prosecution for solar contract misrepresentation; $4.3M civil settlement.

### Sunrun's Own PPA Sets the Duty
Most Sunrun "Sunrun Obligations" clauses (typical 2012 form) say Sunrun will "operate, monitor, maintain, and repair" the System "as Sunrun deems appropriate." Combined with PPA § 8 production guarantee, this creates an **express contractual duty to detect and act on production failures**. Detection without timely disclosure breaches both express terms and the implied covenant.

## Implied Covenant / Tort Theory

When the contract creates an express monitoring obligation and the provider has *exclusive* access to the monitoring data, the *LiMandri* duty-to-disclose framework attaches: exclusive knowledge + partial representations = actionable concealment. (See concealment brief.)

## Comparative Authority

No California appellate case squarely holds a solar provider liable for delayed disclosure of detected production failure (this is a developing area — your case may be **first-impression**, which is a feature for settlement). Closest analogs:

- *Hambrick v. Healthcare Partners* (2015) 238 Cal.App.4th 124 — service provider with superior knowledge owes disclosure.
- *Aas v. Superior Court* (2000) 24 Cal.4th 627, 642 — distinguishes between defects causing economic loss vs. property damage; argue 2017 system continues to fail = ongoing economic loss attributable to monitoring failure.
- Utility-context analog: *Niehaus Bros. v. Contra Costa Water Co.* (1911) 159 Cal. 305, 318 — utility's duty to provide adequate service includes duty to communicate service interruption. Old, but principle still cited.

## Time Benchmarks (See Brief 9)
- *Civ. Code § 1793.2(b)* (Song-Beverly) — repairs within 30 days.
- Industry: NABCEP / SEIA service-level guidance is 30–45 days for service response. **Five months is grossly outside any benchmark.**
