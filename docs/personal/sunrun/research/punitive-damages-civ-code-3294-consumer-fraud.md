## Standard
**Civ. Code § 3294(a):** "[I]n an action for the breach of an obligation not arising from contract, where it is proven by **clear and convincing evidence** that the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice, the plaintiff … may recover damages for the sake of example and by way of punishing the defendant."

**§ 3294(c)(3) "Fraud" defined:** "an intentional misrepresentation, deceit, or **concealment of a material fact known to the defendant** with the intention on the part of the defendant of thereby depriving a person of property or legal rights or otherwise causing injury."

→ This statute is custom-built for the Sunrun fact pattern.

## Corporate Attribution — *White v. Ultramar, Inc.* (1999) 21 Cal.4th 563
**§ 3294(b):** Corporate punitives require "advance knowledge and conscious disregard, authorization, ratification or act of oppression, fraud, or malice … on the part of an officer, director, or **managing agent**."

**Holding:** "managing agent" includes "only those corporate employees who exercise substantial independent authority and judgment in their corporate decision making so that their decisions ultimately determine corporate policy." Position in hierarchy and hire/fire authority not dispositive — *function and policy authority* control.

**Application:** The Service Pipeline Specialist (Katherine Wilson) and whoever maintains Sunrun's "system issue" template language and 67-day no-disclosure protocol qualify if their decisions set the *corporate response policy* for production failures. Discovery target: Sunrun's CSAT/escalation playbook, the Salesforce template that produced "we've identified a system issue," and identity of the manager who drafted/approved it.

## Concealment Specifically Supports Punitives
- *Stevens v. Superior Court* (1986) 180 Cal.App.3d 605 — concealment of known defect supports punitives.
- *Las Palmas Assocs. v. Las Palmas Center Assocs.* (1991) 235 Cal.App.3d 1220, 1239–40 — concealment with intent to harm = malice for § 3294.
- *Weeks v. Baker & McKenzie* (1998) 63 Cal.App.4th 1128 — managing agent ratification can be inferred from corporate inaction after knowledge.

## Recent Cases
- *Major v. Western Home Ins. Co.* (2009) 169 Cal.App.4th 1197, 1224–25 — managing-agent test under *White* applied to mid-level claims supervisors.
- *Roby v. McKesson Corp.* (2009) 47 Cal.4th 686, 715 — § 3294(b) ratification can be by inaction once corporate officers know.
- *CRST, Inc. v. Superior Court* (2017) 11 Cal.App.5th 1255 — punitives upheld where corporation had standardized policy that caused harm.

## Ratio Limits — Due Process
*State Farm v. Campbell* (2003) 538 U.S. 408 — single-digit multiplier presumptively constitutional; higher when conduct is reprehensible. *Simon v. San Paolo U.S. Holding* (2005) 35 Cal.4th 1159 (CA application).
