**Date:** April 17, 2026 | **For:** Sunrun matter, 5 Caladium

## Statutes
- **Civ. Code § 1572(3)** — actual fraud includes "the suppression of that which is true, by one having knowledge or belief of the fact."
- **Civ. Code § 1709** — "One who willfully deceives another with intent to induce him to alter his position to his injury or risk, is liable for any damage which he thereby suffers."
- **Civ. Code § 1710(3)** — deceit includes "the suppression of a fact, by one who is bound to disclose it, or who gives information of other facts which are likely to mislead for want of communication of that fact."

## Elements (CACI 1901)
(1) Concealment/suppression of material fact; (2) duty to disclose; (3) intent to defraud or induce reliance; (4) plaintiff unaware and would have acted differently; (5) resulting damage.

## Duty to Disclose — *LiMandri v. Judkins* (1997) 52 Cal.App.4th 326, 336
Four trigger circumstances: (1) fiduciary; (2) **defendant has exclusive knowledge of material facts not known to plaintiff**; (3) active concealment; (4) **partial representations that suppress other material facts**.

> "Other than the first instance, the other three circumstances in which nondisclosure may be actionable presuppose the existence of some other relationship between the plaintiff and defendant in which a duty to disclose can arise. … Such a relationship can only come into being as a result of some sort of transaction between the parties." (*Id.* 336–37.)

**Application:** Circumstances (2) and (4) are squarely on point. Sunrun's monitoring portal told only Sunrun the system produced zero kWh starting Nov 7, 2025; the consumer-facing channel labeled it a "system issue" — a partial representation suppressing the load-bearing fact (zero generation, six SDG&E billing cycles of 100% grid purchase). The 11/10/25 internal Case #18181148 ("Metering") was never disclosed.

## Best Modern Cases (since 2010)
| Case | Holding |
|---|---|
| *Bigler-Engler v. Breg, Inc.* (2017) 7 Cal.App.5th 276, 311 | Reaffirms LiMandri four-circumstance test. |
| *Hambrick v. Healthcare Partners* (2015) 238 Cal.App.4th 124, 162 | Superior knowledge of service capabilities = disclosure duty. |
| *Collins v. eMachines* (2011) 202 Cal.App.4th 249, 256 | Manufacturer's exclusive knowledge of defect = duty to disclose; warranty disclaimers don't obviate. |
| *Falk v. General Motors* (N.D. Cal. 2007) 496 F.Supp.2d 1088, 1095–97 | Exclusive knowledge of speedometer defect + partial representations = duty; CLRA/UCL omission survived. Persuasive analog for monitoring data. |
| *Daugherty v. American Honda* (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 824, 835 | Concealment must be of fact "central to the function" — solar production is the *only* function of a PPA system. |

## Damages
Out-of-pocket measure under § 3343 + consequentials proximately caused; punitive damages under § 3294 on clear-and-convincing fraud.

## Risks
- *Hoffman v. 162 N. Wolfe LLC* (2014) 228 Cal.App.4th 1178 — must plead which LiMandri prong; plead both (2) and (4) with specificity.
- *Bower v. AT&T Mobility* (2011) 196 Cal.App.4th 1545 — generic "service quality" gripes fail; cure by identifying the precise fact (zero kWh, Case #18181148) and partial statement ("system issue").
