## Framework — Civ. Code §§ 3300, 3301
§ 3300: damages = "the amount which will compensate the party aggrieved for all the detriment proximately caused thereby, or which, in the ordinary course of things, would be likely to result therefrom." § 3301 limits to detriment "clearly ascertainable" and "not too remote."

## *Lewis Jorge Construction Mgmt., Inc. v. Pomona Unified Sch. Dist.* (2004) 34 Cal.4th 960
Two-tier damages framework rooted in *Hadley v. Baxendale*:
- **General damages** flow "from the breach in the ordinary course of events and as the natural and probable consequence of the breach"; recoverable without special foreseeability proof.
- **Special (consequential) damages** require actual or reasonable foreseeability at contract formation.

The court reversed the contractor's $3.1M lost-profits award because lost bonding capacity → lost future contracts was not "the natural and necessary result of the breach of every construction contract involving bonding."

## *Cates Construction, Inc. v. Talbot Partners* (1999) 21 Cal.4th 28
- Implied covenant of good faith **does not** support tort damages outside insurance context for performance bonds.
- But the court reaffirmed that the breaching party owes the *benefit of the bargain* — the value of what was promised.

## "Thing Bargained For and Not Delivered" — Direct Damages

**The bargained-for product in a solar PPA is electricity.** When Sunrun delivers zero kWh, the *direct* damage is the cost of the substitute electricity from SDG&E — this is the *value of the thing bargained for*, not a consequential loss.

### Supporting Authority
- *Brandon & Tibbs v. George Kevorkian Acct. Corp.* (1990) 226 Cal.App.3d 442, 455 — "The cost of completion or the cost of obtaining substitute performance is the appropriate measure of direct damages."
- *Coughlin v. Blair* (1953) 41 Cal.2d 587, 603 — measure of damages where defendant fails to perform is the cost of obtaining the promised performance from another source.
- **Restatement (Second) of Contracts § 347 cmt. b** — "Loss in value … includes the cost or other detriment of obtaining the performance from another source." California courts routinely cite this.
- *McDonald v. John P. Scripps Newspaper* (1989) 210 Cal.App.3d 100, 105 — "cost-of-cover" applies in service contracts when substitute performance is reasonably available.

## Application to 5 Caladium
- **Direct damages = $2,583 documented SDG&E excess + ~$2,500 projected NEM true-up + PPA § 8 refund.** These are the cost of cover for the bargained-for kWh. They are *not* consequentials excluded by the LoL clauses.
- **Consequentials (would be excluded if cap enforced):** lost time/wages, emotional distress (limited in contract), increased SREC value lost, etc. These are the items the LoL caps exclude.
- **NEM true-up** is the most defensible "direct" measure because it is the literal grid replacement of the contracted-for solar production, denominated in kWh × utility rate — a textbook *Brandon & Tibbs* substitute-performance computation.

## Risks
- Sunrun will argue SDG&E excess is consequential because the homeowner "chose" to use grid power. Counter: the homeowner had no choice — solar was zero, and electricity is essential.
- *Erlich v. Menezes* (1999) 21 Cal.4th 543 — limits emotional-distress damages in contract; bypass by pleading concealment/CLRA.
